Assumptions About Input

14 Aug 2016 on Teaching

Two Central - and Erroneous - Assumptions

According to The Lexical Approach, there are two central assumptions about language learning that have been disproven with actual research.

“a. It is possible (and desirable) to sequence language.”

“b. Learning is a step-by-step process, and too much input would confuse.”

Obviously this is wrong. Primary language acquisition isn’t done in careful steps - babies are essentially bombarded with all aspects of a language at once. We do use “motherese” when speaking directly to babies, but we don’t use it anytime a baby can hear us. The television certainly isn’t only speaking in “motherese” either. We also don’t try to shut up around the baby for fear it may become overwhelmed by the language. On the contrary, we speak to it as much as possible. We describe our activities. We name literally everything the baby sees or tries to put in its mouth. And we absolutely know they’re not going to retain all that - yet we don’t dilute the flow of language.

So too is immersion school thought to be the best tool for secondary language acquisition. “Go to a foreign country and you’ll pick up the language so much more quickly!” And yet in a regular language classroom, we are wary to carefully space out grammar and vocabulary and concepts, lets we overwhelm the learners.

This is something I have found myself guilty of in my own classrooms - if a lesson is focused around a particular theme, I often won’t dump a lot more out-of-theme language into it. I have seen kids retain information from the densest of sessions, and also be unable to recall one of the only four vocabulary words they learned in a previous session.

Clearly this should influence my practices, and it’s fascinating to read the theory behind this. The question lies somewhere in the middle, of course. Not all input will be valuable to the learner, so how do we distinguish useful input from extra noise?

There are some factors we cannot control for, of course - they reside in the mindset of the learner. Attention span, tiredness, interest level, motivation, etc.

What we can control:

a) input should be comprehensible

b) input should relate in some way to what is already known and expand upon it (for a good example of this see the Pimmsler tapes and their rather steep learning curve)

In Conclusion

Language should be “roughly tuned” to the needs of the learners. The message should be understandable, and the language used should be below, at, and slightly above the comprehension of the learner. According to Krashen,

“Language acquisition can only take place when a message which is being transmitted is understood, i.e. when the focus is on what is being said rather than the form of the message. This could be referred to as the Great Paradox of Language Teaching.”

Is it paradoxical at all? It makes sense to this teacher that language that is useful and interesting is language that is easier to acquire.
