Vowel Characteristics

24 Aug 2016 on Teaching

Vowels and Their Nonsense

One of the books I’m reading for my DELTA course is on phonetics, phonology, and the sounds of the English language. Having a linguistics degree and spending roughly a billion hours on this stuff already, it’s obvious that I am very excited to read this book.

For some reason, instead of starting with the easier parts of phonetics (consonants), we’re instead starting with the terror of all phonetics students, the dreaded vowels. Why are they terrible? We’ll get to that. First, let’s see what my book has to say about what vowels are/what they do/how we categorize them.

Vowels are sounds made with no obstruction of the airflow. They are always voiced.

Essentially, if your mouth is open the entire time you’re making a sound, and your tongue isn’t touching any part of your mouth, and if your vocal cords are vibrating, it’s a vowel.

We distinguish vowels in four ways:

1) Vertical tongue position. Vowels can be low, mid, or high.

2) Horizontal tongue position. Vowels can be front, mid, or back.

3) Lip position. Vowels can be made with rounded lips, neutral lips, or spread lips.

4) Vowel length. Vowels can be short or wrong.

Now, Why Is This Nonsense?

Vowels aren’t static. They’re not made in the same way across all languages. (Almost) Every language has a version of /p t k/. But even in languages with THE SAME vowels, they might be said differently. In people who speak the same language or even the same regional dialect, vowels can be said differently.

Vowels themselves and the phonemes that mark them aren’t absolutes. They’re signifiers for a sound that’s in an acceptable range of sound quality.

Look at the nonsense that formant charts are if you want to see how difficult it can be to distinguish different vowels.

An addendum: there are even more distinguishing features for vowels, such as the mystifying tense and lax, and adding diphthongs to the mix makes all of this even more muddied.

One More Ridiculous Example

English is not a language that distinguishes between long and short vowels phonemically. Thai is. However, English pronunciation has sneaky phonological rules regarding vowel length. In general, vowels followed by a voiced consonant are longer than vowels followed by a voiceless consonant. Say the following pair of words a few times, playing around with the vowel length.

[hi:t] heat and [hIt] hit

Notice how heat is much longer than hit? and if you make one too long and the other too short it’s much harder to tell the difference? Welcome to reason 83209 why English is such a hard 2nd language to learn. Even when my students are pronouncing (mostly) the word-final consonants in “crab” and “crap”, because of the vowel length, the words are often indistinguishable.

Of course, they rarely pronounce the -b and -p correctly anyway, since word-final consonants are usually devoiced or elided in Thai, and -b and -p aren’t phonemically separate. Hooray English.
