What is a Consonant Though?

22 Aug 2016 on Lexical, Teaching

Definition Problems

There are two definitions for “consonant”, and they both come with their own problems.

Definition one: sounds made by restricting or blocking the airflow in some physical way.

The problem with this definition is that it leaves out the English phonemes /j/ and /w/, which can be found in words like yard and wish, respectively. /j/ and /w/ have tongue positions and are semi-vowels/glides as opposed to proper consonants. Which means we have to bring in another way to distinguish them from vowels.

Definition two: a sound that either alone or in a consonant cluster marks the beginning or end of a syllable.

This makes a lot of sense, because those with Linguistics training know that the vowel is the heart of the syllable. They know that consonants can only start or end syllables, and they know that there is a finite number of consonant clusters and variations that can occur at the beginning or end of any given syllable.

Great! Although:

Unless you’ve HAD a bunch of Linguistics training, you don’t know what sounds ARE allowed to be the beginning/ending of syllables. English has a lot of weird rules.

For instance, we have the voiced velar nasal -ng word finally but not word-initially. That’s not a universal thing, there are plenty of words in Thai that start with ng-. Snake, for example, is ngoo.

We can begin a syllable with str-, spr-, sl-, tr-, and many other two-or-three consonant clusters. But unless you take the time to think about it, this isn’t a very helpful way to determine where the consonants and vowels are.

What I would suggest if I was teaching non-linguists is to say that a consonant is any sound that constricts or stops the airflow in speech OR is /w/ or /j/.

If I was teaching linguists, I would love to go into syllable restraints, stress, vowels, syllabified consonants, and all that fun stuff. But in general, the people I teach are small enough to still get to have naptime after lunch.
