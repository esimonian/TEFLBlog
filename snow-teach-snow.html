Krashen’s Natural Approach and Affective Filter Hypothesis

i.e. why we don’t teach “mittens” to Thai children.

It doesn’t matter a single bit if material is “authentic” or “artificial”: a map of the London Underground is just as “real” to a Thai child as a dialogue from Gilligan’s Island. In order for language to be authentic, it should be applicable.

We teach “mangosteen”, “rambutan”, and “durian” far before we teach “peach” “pear” or “fig”, simply because a Thai child might not have ever seen a peach, and will either way be more likely to shout the English word of a fruit he or she sees every day as opposed to a few times on a flash card.

The affective Filter Hypothesis expands on this and states that:

“input is more likely to become intake if learners feel good, about themselves, about the target language, and about the learning environment. Furthermore… although increasing the stress on students in class may improve their short-term learning, it has negative effects on their long-term acquisition.”

In our practice, we sort of trick the students into learning. It’s the old “hide the pill in the hot dog” trick, which is especially effective for young learners. More broadly, however, it can be applied to almost all the language teaching that we do. A learner that feels confident in their speaking ability, who is praised and given constructive criticism, will not only seek out more opportunities to speak but will actually learn faster.

Jaqueline Steiger
