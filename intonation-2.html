Tones In English, a Non-Tonal Language

While we don’t distinguish tones phonemically, they are an integral part of speech, communication, and semantics. We use tones for emphasis, for humor, for context, for innuendo, and for many parts of metalanguage.

Here’s a few vague types (again, we can’t quantify them so there’s no consensus):

Proclaiming and Referring

Information which is additional to the common ground is marked by a pitch that finishes with a falling movement, and is given the name proclaiming tone. Information which is given as already shared and part of the common ground is marked by a pitch that finishes with a rising movement and is given the name referring tone, since it refers back to something already shared or negotiated.

Think of reffering tone as a “right?” or a “you know?” at the end of the sentence, but already encoded in the tone. And proclaiming tone is just that: proclaiming new information.

Oblique Intonation

Oblique Intonation uses a fallng tone and is used for recitation where language is being quoted, rather than meaning being communicated. Reciting a rhyme, a poem, reading recipes or prayers, etc. It could also be used for reading aloud from a text. It’s basically elementary school read-talk. If you’ve spent any time in a primary-language classroom, almost all “popcorn” readings have the same sort of monotonous cadence.

Zero Tone

Zero tone is a level pitch without significant pitch contrast. Obviously. It’s used for things that aren’t emphasized, whether they’re grammaticalized utterances, or bored speech.
