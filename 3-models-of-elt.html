3 Models of ELT

17 Sep 2016 on Lexical, Teaching

This Is Boring But I Have To Know It

And now you will too.

There seems to be a “natural order” of grammatical morpheme acquisition, and it goes as follows:

-ing (progressive) (noun) plural(s) copula (‘to be’) ↓ auxiliary (progressive as in He is going) article ↓ irregular past ↓ regular past –ed third person –s possessive ’s

Which is actually pretty neat. And there seems to be some evidence of regression - once a student has internalized a rule and goes on to do something else, they might synthesize that old rule incorrectly.

Here are three conflicting theories about language learning:

Behaviourism

With the British “u” and everything. This is based on modeling and imitation, and requires positive reinforcement and corrective strategies. Based off behavioural psychology, which was essentially a “pics or it didn’t happen” branch of psych.

This branch of learning stresses listening, repetition, imitation, and correction, as well as the acquisition of generative structures in a linear fashion.

Obviously the backlash to this produced:

The Innatist Perspective

Dammit, Noam. Stay out of this. So this one is the idea of the Universal Grammar and Generative Grammar and a lot of Noam Nonsense. It’s got good points, in that babies can’t have heard every speech utterance. There has to be SOME sort of internal language system.

Neither of these two systems are perfect, though.

Side Note for Krashen

Krashen is the guy the The Lexical Approach author has a huge hard-on for. He suggests:

“there was an essential difference between learning and acquisition, the former being akin to the conscious learning one does in class, the latter being largely unconscious and at a deeper, more permanent level. It also proposed that acquired language gave rise to spontaneous production (fluency), whereas learned language would be more deliberate, consciously produced, probably for a pedagogical or other formal purpose (accuracy).

A related hypothesis proposed that learners used a monitor to check what the accuracy and appropriateness of what they were saying, sometimes consciously, often unconsciously.”

He also had the idea of the affective filter, the novel concept that things that are engaging and low-stress are easier to learn. Shocking.

Now that we’re in the 21st century and everything has to be goddamn interdisciplinary all the time, we’re moving towards many

Cognitive Perspectives

There’s Information Processing Theory, “the idea of cognitive effort, which in turn involves attention, processing and automatisation”.

There’s Declarative and Proceduarl Learning, concepts that are basically “knowledge that” and “knowledge how”. Thanks, cog sci. This is super groundbreaking.

The Interaction Hypothesis is another one that someone got to claim but is really self-explanatory:

  1. Interactional modification makes input comprehensible 

  2. Comprehensible input promotes acquisition 

  3. Therefore interactional modification promotes acquisition

And then there’s the Sociocultural Approach which says that learning doesn’t happen in a vacuum and prioritizes the social event:

…emphasising the social event rather than the internal goings-on in the mind. Thus, student and tutor, or student and student co-constructed meaning in acting out an interactive event, with the learning arising out of the event itself, and the co-operation between the interactants.

The nature of input was important: it had to be interesting, challenging but not so difficult that it led to frustration. In short, it had to be focused and carefully graded – proximal to the stage of the learner’s stage of development. The theory went that deeper processing occurs when a learner is focused on what they are hearing from their interlocutor and while they are formulating their response. The process of production or output supposedly aids deeper understanding.
