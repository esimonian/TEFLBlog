BRAINCRAM (The Memory)

13 Sep 2016 on Lexical, Teaching

Hack Yo Brain: What Is Memory?

Okay bros. I am about to start acquiring a shit-ton more knowledge about theory and pedagogy. I’m also in the process of trying to learn a new and entirely squiggly language. Thankfully, some of the books I’m reading are literally about HOW TO LEARN or HOW TO TEACH. So lets cross-pollinate these suckers.

Types of Memory

1) The Short Term Store:

This is the brain’s capacity to hold a limited number of items of information for periods of time up to a few seconds.

My brain is AWESOME at this shit. I am a great parrot, and I can remember strings of numbers after hearing them once. Basically, this is the kind of memory you need to repeat a phone number back to someone, or look it up and then dial it. This special memory space is only useful for immediate repetition: it won’t help me remember that ผีเสื้อ (piseua) means butterfly for 30 seconds and then never again. To keep a word, you have to put it into the next brainspace:

2) The Working Memory

Apparently

it can be thought of as a kind of work bench, where information is first placed, studied, and moved about before being filed away for later retrieval.

We can help the working memory out with an articulatory loop, which is basically the tape recorder in your brain repeating ผีเสื้อ… ผีเสื้อ… ผีเสื้อ… while seeing a butterfly. The longer/stronger your articulatory loop is, the easier time you will have learning a new language. I think the articulatory loop is also the secret home of earworms too, though. I have an excellent capacity for lexical retention, and an also excellent capacity for remembering song lyrics to pop songs I super don’t care about but CANNOT GET OUT OF MY HEAD ARGGHHH

Finally, we have the

3) Long Term Memory

which is where we know our name and what a dog looks like and all the stuff that gets to stick in our brain forever.

Okay But Where Are The Hacks

Here’s what you need to learn more things faster:

1) Repetition duh. Unless you anchor the word to a concept or are EXPOSED to the word a bunch, though, it’s just going to stay in the short-term store.

2) Retrieval is being able to think of a word. Once you know ผีเสื้อ, and then SEE a butterfly and shout ผีเสื้อ like a dumbass, you will be much more likely to REMEMBER ผีเสื้อ in the future. Self-reinforcing.

3) Spacing - don’t memorize everything at once. Take some chunks.

4) Pacing - figure out how fast YOU learn and go at that pace. For teachers, figure out how fast your students learn and try to accomodate them if possible.

5) Use - duh again. Use it or lose it.

6) Cognitive depth is how the word is associated in your brain. Words that sound the same are pretty cognitively shallow. If you know synonyms/antonyms that would probably be better. Or part of speech, etc. It’s similar to

7) Personal Organizing - the judgements that you make about the word.

8) Imaging - can you picture the word in your mind? If there’s a picture attached, you learn faster. Nouns (especially non-abstract nouns) or action verbs are easiest to learn. Looks like all the picture flash cards I use in my classes have a purpose after all.

9) Mnemonics aren’t cheating, they’re just another tool in your arsenal. I still remember the phases of mitosis from 7th grade.

10) Motivation - I learned the word for “bathroom” in Thai real damn fast, I tell you what.

11) Attention/Arousal - are you focusing? Is it interesting?

12) Affective Depth - how do YOU feel about it? Is it a pretty word? is it of a delicious food?
