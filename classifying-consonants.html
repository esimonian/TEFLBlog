Classifying Consonants

26 Aug 2016 on Lexical, Teaching

How Do We Distinguish Consonants?

Voicing

Consonants can either be voiced or voiceless, and English comes with paired voiced and voiceless consonants: /p/ /b/, /t/ /d/, /k/ /g/, etc.

Note that English does not distinguish between aspirated and unaspirated consonants (aspirated = a puff of air accompanies the sound as it comes out of your mouth. Put your hand up to your mouth and say “pot” and “spot”. You’ll feel air come out for “pot” but not “spot”) but many other languages do.

Place of Articulation

We talk about consonants based on where in the mouth the airflow is restricted. Consonants that involve the lips are labial or bilabial. Consonants that involve a curled-back tongue are retroflex.

Generally, the places of articulation (FOR ENGLISH) as we move back through the mouth are thus: bilabial, labiodental, alveolar, alveopalatal, velar, glottal.

Fascinating types of sound we don’t have in English: retroflex, palatal, uvular, pharyngeal.

Manner of Articulation

This variable describes the way in which airflow is constricted.

Stops or plosives involve a complete blockage of airflow.

Fricatives involve a narrowing of the airways and generally a hissing sound.

Nasals involve air moving through the nose as well as the mouth.

There’s a lot of types, some of them are really interesting!
