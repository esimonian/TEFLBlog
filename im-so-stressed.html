How Stressed Are You

30 Aug 2016 on Lexical, Teaching

How Stressed Are You?

There are five distinguishable markings of stress in the English language. A stressed syllable can be:

  1. Louder
  2. Longer
  3. Different in pitch (usually higher)
  4. More clearly articulated (and therefore its phonemes sound more “pure”)
  5. Accompanied by larger jaw, lip, and facial movements.

Makes sense. We don’t necessarily use all of these stress markers in every word, but we use a combination of them in every stressed syllable.

My book goes on to say that if a learner is having trouble with stress, we can pick out one or two markers that they find the easiest to work with, and have them focus on those until they get the stress patterns more under control. Neat!

Don’t Stress Out

The vowels of an unstressed syllable are often reduced by Native speakers, something learners might have trouble with.

This is Great

“Some writers emphasize the existence of rules underlying the placement of word stress in English. Sometimes they cite the facility with which a native speaker can correctly stress an unknown word. On the other hand, some writers emphasize the lack of generative power of such rules, citing the difficulty that native speakers can encounter when trying to place the stress in an unknown word.”

Are there stress rules? What are they? IT IS A PUZZLEMENT.

  1. There is a general tendency to stress two-syllable nouns and ajectives on the first syllable.
  2. For words that are both nouns and verbs, the noun version has first-syllable stress and the verb version has second-syllable stress. (present, record)
  3. Compound nouns tend to follow the right-hand head, right-hand stress rule unless the compound formed from two joined or hyphenated words.
