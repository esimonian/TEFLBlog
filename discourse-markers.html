Discourse Markers

28 Aug 2016 on Lexical, Teaching

Meta-language

We have a whole secret category of words that don’t have any semantic meaning. They don’t add to the sentence, they don’t impart any specific meaning regarding the intent of the speaker or writer. The only thing they do is provide information about the STRUCTURE of the sentence/phrase/speech (utterance) itself. We call these discourse markers.

What Do Discourse Markers Do?

They help us organize the flow of information. We can use them to:

1) Signpost logical relationships and sequences - “firstly” “furthermore”, etc

2) Manage conversation and turn-takeing - “and you?”

3) Influence how our listeners or readers react - “all the same”

4) Express our attitude to what we say and write - “I’m afraid”

This is really more of a feeling than a hard and fast category. There’s not a whole lot of consensus or rigidly-defined list of discourse markers. Here are some examples:

Firstly

in fact

on the contrary

however

nevertheless

futhermore

right?

you know

on the other hand

so

anyway

uhh

finally

All of these words/utterances tell us information about the discourse preceding or following it. How does it relate? Where is it in a sequence? How do we feel about it?

Things that discourse markers can do:

a) numbering and order

b) adding something

c) linking similar things together

d) introducing something that contrasts with expectations

e) causes and results

f) generalizing

g) exemplifying and narrowing down

h) re-stating

i) rounding off

j) conversation management

How do we teach something as subjective, varied, and intricate as discourse markers?
